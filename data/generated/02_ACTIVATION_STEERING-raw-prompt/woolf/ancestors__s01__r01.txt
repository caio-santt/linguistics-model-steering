The oak had been a silent witness to the seasons of her life: the first blush of spring when she learned to tie her hair with a ribbon, the harsh winter when her father’s boots left deep prints in the snow. Each bark, each knot was a testament to endurance, a quiet echo of the resilience she had inherited.

The thought of that tree made her feel a sudden warmth, a fragile thread of hope weaving through her thoughts. She recalled the day her mother had taken her to the market, to a stall where a woman sold fresh carnations. The woman had knelt to her, offering a single bloom with a smile that seemed to say, “This is for you.” The scent of the flower lingered in her mind, a perfume of possibility that had lingered long after the petals had wilted.

In the quiet of the evening, she imagined herself walking through that market again, the cobblestones cool beneath her bare feet. She pictured the faces of the vendors, their eyes bright with the simple joy of trade. She remembered the feeling of holding a freshly cut flower in her hand, its thrum of life resonating with her own heartbeat. She felt a sudden, almost imperceptible shift in her perspective; the city was no longer a suffocating maze but a canvas of human kindness and fleeting beauty.

Her thoughts drifted to Jack Renshaw, who had once remarked on her frock, and she realized that his comment, though dismissive, had been an unintentional reminder of how society often reduced a person to mere surface. She felt a surge of gratitude for her mother's quiet teachings, for the way she had learned to see beyond the ordinary and find meaning in the smallest things.
