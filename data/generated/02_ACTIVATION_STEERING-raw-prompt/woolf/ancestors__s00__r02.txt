The night settled over the room like a damp shawl, the fire’s embers glowing a soft amber that barely warmed the air. Mrs. Vallance lifted her tea cup, the porcelain faintly trembling in her fingers. She could hear the distant rumble of traffic, the clatter of the city that never slept, and she felt the weight of the world pressing against the narrow windows. The city’s lights were a pale, unsteady glow, nothing like the bright, clear sky of the Highlands where her father’s laughter had once floated through the air.

Jack Renshaw, who had sat beside her for a while, seemed to be listening to the fire rather than to her words. He shifted his gaze between the swirling steam and the dimly lit windows, his face a mask of gentle curiosity. “You speak of beauty, Mrs. Vallance,” he said quietly, “and yet you sit in a room that feels more like a cage than a sanctuary. Do you not find the city’s bustle a distraction from the soul?”

She smiled, a faint, almost imperceptible line that curled at the corners of her eyes. “It is a distraction indeed,” she replied, “but also a mirror. The city reflects the world in a different light. Just as the Scottish glens reflect the sky, so does London reflect the lives of those who dwell within its walls. Yet, my heart remembers the scent of the loam after rain, the way the wind carried the scent of pine from the hills, the sound of the distant bells of St. Andrew’s, and it is that memory that keeps me tethered to the past.”

Jack nodded, his expression thoughtful. “And you speak of your father, Sir Duncan Clements, and of your mother, Catherine Macdonald, and of the old Greek sage, Mr. Rogers. How do they live in your mind?”

Catherine, my mother, she had a tenderness that could melt the coldest heart.
