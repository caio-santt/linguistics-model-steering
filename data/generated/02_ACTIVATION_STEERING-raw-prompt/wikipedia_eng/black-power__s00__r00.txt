Founded in October 1966 by Huey P. Newton and Bobby Seale in Oakland, California, the Panthers adopted a revolutionary platform that called for armed vigilance, community control of schools, and the establishment of free breakfast programs for children. Their rhetoric echoed the earlier calls for self‑determination, yet it was markedly more radical, framing the struggle as a direct confrontation with the state’s oppressive apparatus. [12] The Panthers’ presence in the streets, often armed with rifles, drew both admiration and fear, prompting the FBI’s COINTELPRO campaign aimed at discrediting and dismantling the organization. [13]

While the Panthers gained notoriety for their militant posture, other factions within the black power movement pursued more culturally centered approaches. The National Black Arts Festival, founded in 1971, sought to showcase African‑American artistic expression, from poetry to visual arts, as a form of resistance against cultural erasure. Simultaneously, the Urban League’s “Black Economic Development” initiative began to finance black-owned cooperatives, providing micro‑loans and business training to entrepreneurs in underserved neighborhoods. These efforts underscored a key tension within the movement: the balance between militant resistance and constructive institution‑building. [14]

The 1970s also witnessed the emergence of the New Left’s “Black Power” wing, which aligned with feminist and environmental activism. Scholars such as Angela Davis argued that true liberation required an intersectional framework that addressed not only racial oppression but also gender, class, and ecological degradation. Davis’s seminal essay, “Women, Race, & Class,” published in 1981, became a touchstone for activists who sought to broaden the scope of black power beyond the confines of the prison-industrial complex. [15]

Critiques of the movement intensified as the 1980s approached. Some argued that the emphasis on separatism alienated potential allies within the broader civil rights coalition, while others contended that the focus on armed self‑defense exacerbated tensions with law enforcement. The 1989 publication “The Myth of Black Separatism” by William A. Leonard challenged the narrative that black power sought isolation, instead positing that it was a strategic response to systemic exclusion. [16] Yet, despite such debates, the legacy of black power endured, influencing subsequent generations of activists who would draw upon its principles in the fight against police brutality and mass incarceration.

In the late 1990s, the rise of the Black Lives Matter movement can be seen as a direct descendant of the black power ethos.
